The Bodensee sits at the junction of Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, and its shores carry 4,000 years of history in an unusually dense stretch of land: Neolithic and Bronze Age villages preserved under the water, the cradle of the modern airship, a monastery island that once produced the finest illuminated manuscripts in Europe, and a city that ended the worst crisis in medieval Christianity and burned a reformer at the stake for good measure. Most visitors come for the views and the wine. You'll come for everything underneath.
Day 1

From a Roman harbor island to Bronze Age pile dwellings to the oldest inhabited castle in Germany
1
Lindau
The island city on the lake
🏘️ Island Town
⏱ 1.5 hours
👶 5/5 baby friendly
A medieval free imperial city on an island in the lake, with a Roman harbor entrance guarded by a stone lion
Lindau is built on a small island connected to the mainland by a bridge, and it has been an important crossing point since the Romans established a garrison here in the 3rd century AD. The harbor entrance — flanked by a 33-meter lighthouse and a stone Bavarian lion — marks what was once one of the most important trading ports on the Bodensee. Lindau was a Free Imperial City from 1275, answerable directly to the Holy Roman Emperor and no one else, a status it held for nearly 600 years.

The old town is extraordinarily intact: the Peterskirche dates to the 10th century, the Diebsturm (Thieves' Tower) to the 13th, and the stepped-gable Rathaus to 1422. In 1530, Lindau's delegation to the Diet of Augsburg signed the Augsburg Confession alongside the Protestant princes — one of the founding documents of the Lutheran Reformation.
Walk to the northern tip of the island to the Heidenmauer — a stretch of Roman wall that most visitors walk past without realizing it predates the medieval town by a thousand years. For coffee with a view, skip the harbor cafés entirely and drive up to Café Hoyerberg on the mainland hill: the panorama over the island, the Austrian Alps across the water, and the Swiss peaks beyond is better than anything on the island itself, and almost entirely local.
Lindau is one of the most stroller-friendly stops on this trip — the island is flat, main streets are paved, and there are benches everywhere. Changing facilities in the public toilets near the harbor. Park on the mainland at Parkhaus Inselbahnhof and walk across — the island parking fills by 10am.

Zeppelin Museum Friedrichshafen
The world's greatest airship museum
🏛️ Technical Museum
⏱ 2 hours
👶 5/5 baby friendly
A full-scale recreation of the passenger gondola of the Hindenburg — with original furniture intact — in the city where it was built
Ferdinand von Zeppelin was born near Konstanz in 1838 and spent thirty years as a cavalry general before retiring at 52 and devoting the rest of his life to building rigid airships. The LZ 1 made its first flight over the Bodensee in 1900. By the 1930s, the LZ 127 Graf Zeppelin had made 590 flights including 144 transatlantic crossings. The LZ 129 Hindenburg — 245 meters long, the largest flying object ever built — made 36 Atlantic crossings before the fire at Lakehurst, New Jersey in 1937. The disaster ended the airship era within a year.

The museum holds the most important Zeppelin collection in the world, including a full-scale recreation of the Hindenburg's passenger section: the dining room, the observation lounge, the windows over the ocean. The engineering of these objects is almost incomprehensible — a rigid aluminum skeleton 245 meters long, covered in cotton fabric, filled with hydrogen, carrying 50 passengers across the Atlantic in 60 hours.
Most visitors spend all their time in the Zeppelin hall and skip the fine art collection upstairs — a serious mistake. The upper floor has one of the strongest collections of Expressionist and Neue Sachlichkeit art in southern Germany, including major works by Otto Dix and Max Beckmann. The Dornier Museum is a 10-minute walk away and covers the flying boat manufacturer also based here. Together they make Friedrichshafen one of the most important aviation history sites in Europe — and almost no one knows either exists.
Fully accessible throughout — elevator between floors, flat exhibition spaces, wide corridors. There is a good café on the ground floor with high chairs. The lakefront promenade directly outside is flat and perfect for a post-museum walk. Baby changing room in the main toilets.

Pfahlbaumuseum Unteruhldingen
Bronze Age villages, rebuilt over the water
🏛️ Open-Air Museum · UNESCO
⏱ 1.5 hours
👶 4/5 — carrier easier on walkway
Walk through reconstructed Neolithic and Bronze Age homes built over the lake — where the 4,000-year-old originals still lie under the sediment
When Lake Constance water levels dropped in the 1850s, fishermen began finding extraordinarily well-preserved objects in the lake sediment near Unteruhldingen — tools, textiles, leather, food remains, entire structural timbers — dating back 4,000 to 6,000 years. The anaerobic conditions under the lake had preserved organic material that would have rotted anywhere else on Earth within decades. What emerged was evidence of Neolithic and Bronze Age communities who built their villages on wooden platforms over the shallow water.

The open-air museum reconstructs these settlements with extraordinary accuracy — you walk through Bronze Age homes, see the hearths, storage vessels, tools. The lake sediment around the museum contains the actual original site, designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2011 jointly with 110 other Alpine pile dwelling sites across six countries. This is one of the most important prehistoric archaeological sites in Central Europe, and almost no one outside Germany has heard of it.
The museum boat trip gives you the view from the water and a proper sense of how these villages related to the lake — it runs on the hour, takes 20 minutes, and is worth catching. The underwater archaeology exhibition inside the main building shows actual objects retrieved from the sediment: preserved leather shoes, wooden ladles, fabrics that are real and 4,000 years old. More affecting than any reconstruction.
The wooden walkway over the water is narrow in places — a stroller works but requires care on the turns; a carrier is more comfortable. The indoor exhibition hall is fully accessible. There is a lakeside terrace café with high chairs. One changing room near the entrance.

Meersburg Altes Schloss
The oldest inhabited castle in Germany
🏰 7th-Century Castle
⏱ 1.5 hours
👶 2/5 — steep town, carrier required
A castle with parts dating to the 7th century, where Germany's greatest female poet spent her final years and died in 1848
The Altes Schloss in Meersburg is one of the oldest continuously inhabited castles in Germany — parts of the Dagobertsturm may date to the Merovingian period in the early 7th century. For nearly 700 years, from 1526 to 1803, it served as the residence of the Prince-Bishops of Konstanz. When Napoleon's reorganization of Germany dissolved the bishopric, the castle passed into private hands.

In 1838 it was purchased by Baron von Laßberg, who invited his sister-in-law to convalesce here: Annette von Droste-Hülshoff, considered Germany's greatest female poet and the face of the old 20-Deutschmark note. She lived the final decade of her life in the castle's tower rooms, writing some of her most important work overlooking the lake, including the crime novella Die Judenbuche. She died in the castle in 1848. Her rooms are preserved exactly as she left them.
The Staatsweingut Meersburg — the oldest state-owned winery in Germany — runs directly down the hillside below the castle to the lake. You can buy bottles at the winery shop for well below restaurant prices; the Grauburgunder is particularly good. The view from the Schlossplatz terrace looking west over the lake and vineyard toward the Swiss Alps is one of the finest in the region. For dinner, Winzerstube zum Becher in the old town is where locals eat — dark wood, lake fish, and a wine list entirely from the local vineyard.
Meersburg is the most challenging stop on this trip for a stroller. The old town is built on a steep hillside with stone steps between levels. The Altes Schloss involves stairs throughout — a baby carrier is necessary. The lakefront promenade at the bottom of the hill is completely flat and a good place to settle the baby while one parent visits the castle. Park at the lower Parkplatz and access the lakefront directly.

5
Reichenau Island
The manuscript island
🏛️ UNESCO World Heritage
⏱ 1.5 hours
👶 5/5 — completely flat
Three Romanesque churches on a small island — the oldest contains 1,000-year-old Ottonian frescoes, still the finest in existence
Reichenau Island was founded as a Benedictine monastery in 724 AD by Saint Pirmin. Within two centuries it had become one of the most important intellectual centers in Europe. The scriptorium at Reichenau produced illuminated manuscripts of extraordinary quality — the so-called Reichenau School of manuscript painting, working in the late 10th and early 11th centuries, created works that influenced religious art across the continent.

The island has three surviving Romanesque churches. The most important is St. Georg in Oberzell, built in the 890s and containing the most complete cycle of Ottonian frescoes in Germany, painted around 1000 AD. Standing in St. Georg in front of those paintings in an almost empty church is one of the genuinely quiet experiences of medieval history in southern Germany. The island was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2000.
Almost every visitor goes to the main Münster near the causeway and skips St. Georg. St. Georg with the frescoes is at the far western end of the island — 15 minutes by bike or car from the causeway. The key is kept in the house opposite and access is free. The island also has the oldest continuously cultivated vegetable gardens in Germany; the farm stands along the road sell directly. Buy the Reichenau tomatoes and cucumbers.
The island is completely flat — designed as a cycling island, with well-maintained paths running its entire length. A stroller works perfectly everywhere. The churches have cool stone interiors. The Münster has a café nearby with outdoor seating. Very quiet and unhurried — one of the most relaxed stops on the route.

Konstanz
The city that ended the Great Schism and survived the bombs
🏘️ Medieval City
⏱ 2.5 hours
👶 4/5 baby friendly
The city that kept its lights on during WWII to be mistaken for Switzerland — and emerged with one of the most intact medieval old towns in Germany
Konstanz sits with its old town literally on the Swiss border. This geography saved it in WWII: the city kept its lights on at night to be visually indistinguishable from neutral Switzerland, and Allied bombers — under orders not to bomb neutral territory — passed over it. The result is one of the most intact medieval old towns in Germany, while cities like Freiburg were destroyed.

The historical weight is considerable. The Council of Constance (1414–1418) was convened here to resolve the Western Schism — a crisis in which three men simultaneously claimed to be the true Pope. Over four years, cardinals and princes from across Europe negotiated in Konstanz. They ended the schism, elected a single new pope (Martin V), and in 1415 condemned the Czech reformer Jan Hus — who had been promised safe conduct — and burned him at the stake for heresy. The Konzilgebäude on the harbor, where the cardinals deliberated, still stands. The harbor also features the Imperia, a 9-meter rotating bronze statue of a courtesan holding miniature figures of the Pope and Emperor as puppets — a deliberate provocation installed in 1993.
The spot where Jan Hus was burned — a small plaque on a quiet street in the Paradies neighborhood northwest of the old town — is almost never visited and worth finding. The Niederburg district between the Münster and the Rhine is the most intact medieval residential quarter and where locals actually live. Cross the border into Switzerland on foot from the old town — you can literally walk across, passport in pocket.
Most of the old town is stroller-friendly — main streets are paved and reasonably flat. The harbor area is completely flat with a wide promenade. Baby changing facilities in the Lago shopping center near the train station. Plenty of cafés with outdoor seating along the harbor.
